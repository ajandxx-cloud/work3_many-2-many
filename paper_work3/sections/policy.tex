\section{Policy Implications}\label{sec:policy}

The numerical experiments in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} provide
simulation-based guidance for DRT deployment decisions in Chinese cities.
Bidirectional meeting point systems offer substantial vehicle efficiency
gains, but their viability depends critically on walking tolerance, fleet
supply, and demand density. This section translates the experimental
findings into five scenario-specific insights for DRT operators and urban
planners, framed for the low-density suburban contexts where DRT is most
commonly deployed in China.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{R1: Walking Radius Threshold is Scenario-Specific}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The sensitivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) shows that
FullModel acceptance rate is \textbf{0.0\%} at $\rho \leq 400$\,m and
rises to \textbf{9.0\%} at $\rho = 1000$\,m. Below this threshold in the tested setup,
the binary logit utility penalty for walking is so large that no meeting-point offer
clears the outside option, and the bidirectional system delivers no
efficiency benefit over door-to-door service. At $\rho = 1000$\,m, FullModel vehicle-km is 219.7 versus 1693.8 for
DoorToDoor in the standard density tier. This reduction in total vehicle-km
partly reflects the lower served share (9.0\% vs.\ 58\%) and should not be
interpreted as an equal-coverage efficiency gain.

\paragraph{Policy implication.}
DRT operators in Chinese cities should treat this as a local calibration question and conduct walking tolerance surveys
before deploying bidirectional meeting points. Districts where residents
routinely walk more than 1000\,m to transit stops --- common in suburban
areas with sparse bus networks --- are prime candidates. Districts with
elderly-dominant populations or poor pedestrian infrastructure (inadequate
lighting, missing footpaths) should retain door-to-door service. Operators
should pilot bidirectional meeting points with opt-in enrollment, allowing
passengers to self-select based on their own walking tolerance before
system-wide rollout. The 1000\,m value should be treated as a simulation
threshold under our parameterization, not as a universal minimum radius.

\paragraph{Generalizability caveat (placed before recommendation).}
The 1000\,m threshold is specific to our synthetic scenario: a $20 \times 20$\,km
service area with a $10 \times 10$ meeting point grid at 2\,km spacing, uniform spatial
demand, and binary logit utility parameters as specified in
Equations~\ref{eq:beta-price}--\ref{eq:beta-walk} (see also
Table~\ref{tab:passenger-types} and Section~\ref{subsec:vot-mapping}).
The 1000\,m value emerges from the interaction of three experimental factors:
(a) the meeting point spacing of 2\,km (nearest MP $\leq$ 1414\,m), (b) the specific
$\beta_1$ values, and (c) the $U_0 = 0$ outside option normalization. Changing any
of these factors would shift the threshold.
Real-world walking tolerance depends critically on pedestrian infrastructure
(lighting, footpath continuity, crossing safety), user demographics (age, mobility),
and local land use (weather exposure, shade, gradient).
In contexts with superior pedestrian infrastructure or a younger, more active user
base, a lower threshold may suffice; in contexts with adverse infrastructure or
elderly-dominant populations, even 500\,m may be excessive.
Operators should conduct local walking tolerance surveys --- or re-calibrate the
$\beta_1$ parameter against observed acceptance data --- before applying this
threshold to a specific deployment.
This finding should be treated as a scenario-specific managerial insight,
not a universal prescription.

\subsection{R2: Fleet Ratio Insight from the Synthetic Peak-Period Scenario}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evidence.}
Fleet size sensitivity analysis shows that FullModel acceptance peaks at
\textbf{24.0\%} with $n_{\text{vehicles}} = 30$ for 200 requests (15.0
vehicles per 100 requests). Diminishing returns set in beyond 20 vehicles:
the marginal acceptance gain per additional vehicle drops sharply after this
point. At $n_{\text{vehicles}} = 15$ (baseline), DoorToDoor acceptance
exceeds FullModel by \textbf{43 percentage points} (61.0\% vs.\ 18.3\%), confirming that
door-to-door service is more robust under vehicle scarcity because it does
not impose walking penalties on passengers.

\paragraph{Policy implication.}
In the tested 200-request synthetic horizon, the high-fleet endpoint of
15 vehicles per 100 requests gives the highest FullModel acceptance. This
should be interpreted as a stress-test insight rather than a budgeting rule.
A system with too few
vehicles will achieve lower acceptance than a simple door-to-door service,
while still imposing walking burdens on passengers. Operators can phase in
vehicles incrementally, using the sensitivity curve (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity})
to project service quality at each fleet size under locally observed demand.
Bidirectional meeting points deliver their greatest advantage when fleet supply
is sufficient to offset the acceptance penalty from walking.

\paragraph{Generalizability caveat (placed before recommendation).}
The 15-vehicles-per-100-requests ratio is derived from a synthetic
scenario with 200 requests over a 4-hour planning horizon, uniform
spatial demand on a $20 \times 20$\,km grid, and 8-passenger vehicles.
It should be treated as an order-of-magnitude guideline, not a universal planning norm.
With 200 requests served by 30 vehicles over a 4-hour horizon, the average
load is approximately 6.7 requests per vehicle (200/30), or 1.7 requests per
vehicle per hour on average. For full-day planning, operators should scale
based on hourly demand patterns rather than applying the ratio directly to daily totals.
Actual fleet requirements depend on demand clustering (concentrated demand can be
served with fewer vehicles than dispersed demand at the same total request count),
temporal patterns (peak-hour concentration vs.\ uniform arrivals), vehicle
capacity, and local walking tolerance.
In high-density corridors with spatially concentrated demand, the effective ratio
may be lower; in dispersed suburban scenarios with low demand density, it may be
substantially higher.
Operators should use the fleet size sensitivity curve (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity})
as a calibration tool with locally observed demand patterns rather than applying
the 15-per-100 figure directly.

\subsection{R3: Monitor Time-Sensitive Passengers for Service Equity}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evidence.}
Equity analysis across three passenger types shows that time-sensitive
passengers have the lowest acceptance rate (\textbf{14.4\%}), compared to
price-sensitive (\textbf{25.4\%}) and walk-sensitive (\textbf{20.2\%})
passengers. Time-sensitive passengers face longer in-vehicle travel times
(avg IVT 1109.6\,s) and higher waiting times (avg 603.2\,s) relative to
other types, making meeting-point offers less attractive under their utility
function. The Gini coefficient of per-type acceptance rates is
\textbf{0.1216}, indicating moderate but policy-relevant inequality.

\paragraph{Policy implication.}
DRT operators should track per-type acceptance rates in real deployments.
If time-sensitive passengers are disproportionately rejected, operators
should consider: (a) adjusting utility weights to reduce the penalty
for this type; (b) offering a door-to-door fallback option for passengers
who opt out of meeting-point service; or (c) subsidizing the disadvantaged
type through reduced fares or priority dispatch. Regulatory bodies in
Chinese cities should require DRT operators to report disaggregated
acceptance rates by passenger segment as a condition of operating licenses.
This aligns with TR Part A's equity mandate for public transit policy and
provides a measurable accountability mechanism for service providers.

\subsection{R4: Match Service Mode to City Density Tier}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The standard scenario (200 requests, 15 vehicles) shows FullModel
vehicle-km = 552.0 versus DoorToDoor = 2603.7, a \textbf{78.8\%} reduction
in total vehicle-km. This total-distance comparison is coverage-confounded
because FullModel serves fewer trips; the equal-coverage comparison in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} should be used for efficiency claims. The
pattern is also density-dependent: at low demand levels
(100 requests per 4-hour horizon in a $20\,\text{km} \times 20\,\text{km}$
service zone), FullModel acceptance approaches 0\% at $\rho = 500$\,m,
while DoorToDoor remains viable. The three-tier framework below should be
read as an illustrative deployment heuristic for the tested simulation setup.

\paragraph{Policy implication.}
An illustrative three-tier deployment framework for the tested setting is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tier 1} ($>$300 requests per 4-hour horizon in the service zone): Bidirectional
        meeting points with binary logit choice model. Demand density is
        sufficient to generate spatially compatible request clusters, and
        the vehicle-km reductions may justify
        the system complexity and passenger walking burden.
  \item \textbf{Tier 2} (100--300 requests per 4-hour horizon): Single-sided pickup meeting
        points with door-to-door dropoff. This hybrid mode captures
        approximately half the vehicle-km savings of full bidirectional
        assignment while reducing the walking burden to pickup only.
  \item \textbf{Tier 3} ($<$100 requests per 4-hour horizon): Door-to-door service.
        Meeting point overhead exceeds efficiency gains at low demand
        levels; the system cannot generate enough spatially compatible
        clusters to justify passenger walking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{R5: Deploy Rolling Horizon Re-optimization for Dynamic Demand}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The AblationNoRollingHorizon experiment shows a \textbf{16.6\%} increase in
vehicle-km when rolling horizon re-optimization is disabled (668.5 vs.\
552.0\,vkm). More critically, average waiting time rises from 454.2\,s
(FullModel) to \textbf{3114.5\,s} without rolling horizon --- a 6.9-fold
increase. Static greedy assignment cannot correct early routing decisions
as new requests arrive, leading to cascading delays and inefficient vehicle
positioning.

\paragraph{Policy implication.}
DRT operators should implement rolling horizon re-optimization with a
planning window $H \geq 30$ minutes and a re-optimization time step
$\Delta = 5$ minutes. The computational overhead is acceptable: average
decision time per request remains below 1 second on instances with 200
requests and 15 vehicles, making real-time deployment feasible on standard
server hardware. Chinese city operators should plan for cloud-based dispatch
systems rather than on-vehicle computation, enabling centralized
re-optimization across the full fleet. Static assignment (greedy insertion
without re-optimization) performed poorly in our dynamic-demand simulation and
should be treated cautiously in production deployments of bidirectional DRT.

\paragraph{Deployment summary.}
Operators should assess three factors before choosing a service mode:
(1) walking tolerance of the target population; (2) fleet supply relative
to peak-period demand; and (3) demand density in requests per horizon and
per service area. In our simulation, non-zero bidirectional acceptance emerged
at $\rho = 1000$\,m and improved with larger fleets, but these values require
local recalibration before deployment. The three-tier framework in R4 and the deployment map in
Figure~\ref{fig:policy-map} provide a practical decision tool for Chinese
city DRT planners navigating these trade-offs.

\subsection{Objective Weight Interpretation via Value of Time}\label{subsec:vot-mapping}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The objective weights $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_4$ in Equation~(\ref{eq:objective})
are dimensionless scaling parameters. The rejection penalty $\Gamma$ in the social
welfare metric (Equation~\ref{eq:welfare}) is a separate post-hoc policy parameter.
To support policy interpretation, we map the routing weights to monetary equivalents
using the \emph{value of time} (VOT) framework standard in transport economics.
The VOT for each service attribute is the marginal willingness to pay (in CNY per
minute) to reduce that attribute by one unit, holding utility constant.

For the binary logit utility function (Equation~\ref{eq:utility}), the implied VOT for
passenger type $k$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vot-formula}
  \text{VOT}_{\text{wait}}^k = \frac{|\beta_2^k|}{|\beta_4^k|}, \quad
  \text{VOT}_{\text{IVT}}^k  = \frac{|\beta_3^k|}{|\beta_4^k|}, \quad
  \text{VOT}_{\text{walk}}^k = \frac{|\beta_1^k|}{|\beta_4^k|} \cdot v_w,
\end{equation}
where $v_w = 80$\,m/min is the assumed walking speed and
$\text{VOT}_{\text{walk}}^k$ converts from CNY/meter (the natural ratio
of $\beta_1$ in utility/meter and $\beta_4$ in utility/CNY) to CNY/min.

Table~\ref{tab:vot-mapping} reports the implied VOT for each passenger type
alongside the Chinese urban VOT benchmarks from \citet{shao2017} and
\citet{li2020drt}.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The walk-sensitive type's implied wait VOT (0.80\,CNY/min) and IVT VOT
(0.40\,CNY/min) fall within the Chinese urban benchmark ranges of 0.8--1.2
and 0.2--0.4\,CNY/min respectively, confirming that the parameter calibration
is behaviorally plausible for the target population \citep{shao2017,li2020drt}.
The time-sensitive type's high wait VOT (3.33\,CNY/min) reflects the extreme
schedule rigidity of commuters with fixed appointments; this is consistent with
peak-hour commuter surveys in Chinese cities \citep{shao2017}.
The price-sensitive type's low wait and IVT VOT reflects passengers who
prioritise fare savings over time, consistent with the low-income suburban
DRT user profile documented in \citet{li2020drt}.

The walk VOT values for all three types (2.67, 13.33, and 32.00\,CNY/min)
exceed the literature walking VOT range of 0.4--0.6\,CNY/min. These high values
reflect the strong walking disutility assumed in the calibration: particularly
for the walk-sensitive type ($\beta_1^{\text{walk}} = -0.020$\,utils/meter),
the coefficient implies an extremely high walking time valuation once converted
through walking speed (80\,m/min). This is a calibration limitation: the
$\beta_1$ values were chosen to represent qualitative ordering among passenger
types rather than calibrated against revealed-preference data.
For types with low price sensitivity (low $|\beta_4|$), the implied walk VOT
is further inflated. The wait and IVT VOT values (0.27--3.33 and 0.13--2.67\,CNY/min
respectively) are the primary policy-relevant benchmarks; the walk VOT should
be treated as an upper bound pending empirical calibration. Accordingly, the
walking-radius threshold, equity gap, and type-specific acceptance rates reported
above should be read as calibration-dependent numerical illustrations rather than
transferable behavioral estimates. This limitation is discussed further in
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

The objective weights $\alpha_2$ (waiting) and $\alpha_3$ (walking) in the
baseline configuration ($\alpha_2 = \alpha_3 = 1.0$) implicitly weight these
attributes equally in vehicle-km equivalent units. Operators who wish to
prioritise service equity for walk-sensitive passengers should increase
$\alpha_3$ relative to $\alpha_1$; the weight sensitivity analysis in
Section~\ref{subsec:weight-sensitivity} shows that qualitative conclusions
are robust across a range of weight configurations.
