    ```latex
    \bigskip\hrule\bigskip

    \section*{v3.0 Revisions (Second Round)}

    Following an internal review of the v2.0 manuscript, we made five additional
    corrections and additions. These changes do not alter any core theoretical
    results or experimental findings from v2.0; they clarify metric definitions,
    add formal statements that were implicit, and reframe two policy findings to
    be appropriately scenario-specific.

    \subsection*{FIX-01: Metric Correction (vkm/trip denominator)}

    The v2.0 manuscript reported vehicle efficiency as vehicle-km per unit acceptance
    \emph{rate} (i.e., vkm~$/$~$\hat\alpha$), which is dimensionally incorrect
    (km per fraction, not km per trip). The correct denominator is the accepted trip
    count $n \cdot \hat\alpha$. With $n = 200$ requests:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Corrected FullModel: $628.5 / (200 \times 0.208) = \mathbf{15.1}$\,vkm/trip
            (previously reported as 3022)
      \item Corrected DoorToDoor: $2603.7 / (200 \times 0.610) = \mathbf{21.3}$\,vkm/trip
            (previously reported as 4268)
      \item The percentage improvement (29.2\%) is unchanged because the $n = 200$
            factor cancels in the ratio.
    \end{itemize}
    All efficiency numbers in the manuscript (Table~1, Section~5.2, abstract) have
    been corrected to use the dimensionally consistent denominator.

    \subsection*{FIX-02: Matched-Coverage Experiment}

    To address the concern that FullModel's lower vkm/trip is partly explained by
    lower served share (20.8\% vs.\ 61.0\% for DoorToDoor), we add a matched-coverage
    comparison that controls for served share. We calibrate DoorToDoor's rejection
    fraction so its served share matches FullModel's mean served share of
    $\approx$23.5\%. At equal coverage:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item FullModel: \textbf{10.9}\,vkm/trip
      \item DoorToDoor (matched): \textbf{42.3}\,vkm/trip
      \item Improvement at equal coverage: \textbf{74.3\%}
    \end{itemize}
    This is now the primary efficiency claim in Section~5.2 and the abstract.
    The unconstrained comparison (15.1 vs.\ 21.3, 29.2\%) is retained as a
    secondary reference.

    \subsection*{FIX-03: Timing/Decision Diagram}

    We add a new timing table (Table~5 in the revised manuscript) to Section~5
    (Three-Layer Coupled Model, Layer Coupling Summary subsection). The table shows
    the event sequence within a single planning cycle: Layer~1 selects $b^*$ given
    current routes; Layer~2 samples $z_r \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(P_{\text{accept}}(b^*))$;
    Layer~3 updates routes and triggers periodic re-optimization at
    $t = 0, \Delta, 2\Delta, \ldots$. The Bernoulli sampling step --- which was
    present in Algorithm~1 but not in the model section --- is now shown explicitly
    with its timing relative to bundle selection.

    \subsection*{FIX-04: ALNS Online Objective Statement}

    We add an \textbf{Online Objective} paragraph to Section~6.2 (Rolling Horizon
    ALNS subsection), before the Candidate Generation step. The paragraph:
    (a)~formally states the surrogate objective
    $\mathbb{E}[\Delta C \mid b, \mathbf{x}_r] = P_{\text{accept}}(b^*) \times
    \Delta C_{\text{routing}}$; (b)~clarifies that Bernoulli sampling occurs
    \emph{after} bundle selection (ALNS uses expected cost, not realized cost, to
    rank bundles); and (c)~confirms that the rejection penalty $\Gamma$ enters only
    the social welfare metric $W$ and does not alter the binary logit probability or
    the routing objective.

    \subsection*{FIX-05: Policy Thresholds Reframed as Scenario-Specific}

    The 1000\,m walking threshold (R1) and the 15-vehicles-per-100-requests fleet
    ratio (R2) were stated in v2.0 without explicit caveats on generalizability.
    We add a \textbf{Generalizability caveat} paragraph to each recommendation:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{R1 caveat}: The 1000\,m threshold is specific to a
            $20 \times 20$\,km synthetic grid with MNL parameters calibrated to
            Chinese suburban conditions. Real-world walking tolerance depends on
            pedestrian infrastructure, demographics, and land use. Operators should
            conduct local walking tolerance surveys before applying this threshold.
      \item \textbf{R2 caveat}: The 15-per-100 ratio is derived from uniform
            synthetic demand and should be treated as an order-of-magnitude
            guideline. Actual fleet requirements depend on demand clustering,
            temporal patterns, and local walking tolerance.
    \end{itemize}
    These findings are now framed as scenario-specific managerial insights rather
    than universal prescriptions.
    ```
